# LIFTIQ Renaming Risk Review

Capture date: 2026-05-14

Status: attorney-supporting naming screen only. This is not legal advice and does not state that any name is available, registrable, or safe to use.

Migration update: DispatchTalon has been selected as the preferred working replacement name for the brand-transition implementation. This selection does not claim legal clearance or trade mark protection.

## 1. Executive Verdict

The current product name, LIFTIQ, should be treated as a high-risk name for public scale until reviewed by a trade mark attorney.

The risk is not the word "lift" by itself. The material issue is the user-supplied evidence that existing registered/protected Australian "LIFTIQ" marks appear in directly relevant software/SaaS classes. IP Australia class guidance confirms that Class 9 includes downloadable software / information technology equipment and Class 42 includes computer programming and non-downloadable software services. Those classes align closely with the current product: a pilot-stage SaaS dispatch intelligence platform.

Recommended action:

1. Stop expanding the LIFTIQ mark publicly until attorney review.
2. Continue using "Pressure Systems" as the stable parent brand.
3. Screen a replacement product name that avoids "Lift", "IQ", and "LIFTIQ-like" construction.
4. Prefer coined industrial names that can cover labour-only and plant + labour allocation without implying safety, compliance, permit approval, or lift engineering.

## 3. Current Name Risk Assessment

Current name: LIFTIQ

Risk estimate: High risk / requires clearance.

Reasoning:

- The product is software/SaaS.
- User-provided screenshots indicate existing/protected LIFTIQ marks in Australian Classes 9 and 42.
- IP Australia guidance confirms Classes 9 and 42 directly cover software / non-downloadable software services.
- LIFTIQ combines "Lift" and "IQ", both common software-style naming elements, and is close to "Lift IQ".
- The current product has crane/lifting context, which makes the "Lift" element directly descriptive or suggestive.
- The product has moved beyond private prototype status into public website, portal, sales, and advertising assets, increasing naming exposure.

Practical conclusion:

- LIFTIQ can remain a temporary internal reference while the replacement is screened.
- Do not invest further in public LIFTIQ-specific brand assets until attorney guidance.
- Do not file or promote LIFTIQ without attorney advice.

## 4. Product Architecture Naming Requirements

The replacement name must cover:

- Labour-only allocation businesses.
- Plant + labour operators.
- Crane, rigging, plant, transport, and lifting operations.
- Dispatch intelligence, not autonomous dispatch.
- Structured job intake, requirements, credentials, fatigue, schedule, asset context, audit, and review flags.
- Pilot-stage and managed pilot positioning.

The name must not imply:

- Compliance approval.
- Permit approval.
- Legal road access.
- Engineered lift confirmation.
- Autonomous dispatch.
- Payroll, invoicing, ERP, maintenance, telematics, GPS, or full fleet management.

## 5. Naming Strategy

Recommended strategy: parent-backed coined industrial product name.

Pattern:

> Pressure Systems [ProductName]

Example:

> Pressure Systems DispatchTalon

Reasoning:

- Keeps Pressure Systems as the stable trust layer.
- Allows product rename without changing the parent business.
- Avoids crane-only or labour-only limitation.
- Gives the attorney a more distinctive coined mark to screen.
- Keeps the product category explainable through the descriptor: "dispatch intelligence for labour and lifting operations".

Recommended descriptor:

> Dispatch intelligence for labour and lifting operations.

Do not make the descriptor the mark. "Dispatch intelligence" is descriptive and should support the product name, not replace it.

## 6. Naming Territories Reviewed

### A. Pressure-native

Examples: PressureOps, PressureGrid, PressureControl, PressureFlow, PressureBoard.

Assessment: Strong parent-brand connection, but several names are generic or broad. "Pressure" can work as house-brand linkage, but product marks like PressureOps may become too close to a service line rather than a distinct product. Better as architecture labels than top product mark.

### B. Dispatch / allocation

Examples: DispatchTalon, DispatchKeel, DispatchGrid, Allocore, JobGrid.

Assessment: Best commercial clarity. "Dispatch" explains the market quickly. Generic pairings like DispatchGrid are crowded. Coined pairings like DispatchTalon and DispatchKeel have better distinctiveness.

### C. Readiness / decision

Examples: DispatchReady, OpsReady, DecisionGrid, JobReady.

Assessment: Useful conceptually, but "Ready" risks weak distinctiveness and may sound like a safety/readiness assurance if not handled carefully. Avoid as primary mark unless attorney sees a strong path.

### D. Heavy operations

Examples: RigGrid, LoadTalon, SiteKeel, PlantTalon.

Assessment: Good industrial credibility. Risk is that crane/plant-heavy terms may under-serve labour-only mode. Works best for secondary candidates or product modules.

### E. Neutral enterprise

Examples: FieldKeel, FieldGrid, ControlGrid, WorkCore.

Assessment: Scalable but can drift toward generic field-service SaaS. FieldKeel is a strong exception because it is distinctive, operational, and broader than crane-only.

## 7. Candidate Scoring Rules

Scores are 1 to 10.

- Dist: distinctiveness.
- Fit: fit for current product.
- Parent: fit under Pressure Systems.
- Ind: industrial credibility.
- Pron: pronunciation and spelling.
- Domain: apparent domain potential from light screening.
- TMR: trade mark risk estimate, where 10 means lower-risk candidate and 1 means high-risk in this light screen.
- Scale: future scalability.
- Mem: memorability.
- Mig: migration ease from LIFTIQ.

Risk band language:

- Lower risk candidate: better initial screen, still requires clearance.
- Medium risk: plausible but needs careful screening.
- Higher risk: crowded, descriptive, or close to existing category terms.
- High risk: avoid unless attorney specifically advises otherwise.

### 3. FieldKeel

Best for: broadest future architecture.

Why:

- Covers field labour, plant, jobs, transport review, asset references, and audit.
- Distinctive enough to feel like a product mark.

- Less exposed to direct dispatch/labour/fleet generic wording.

Risk notes:

- Requires more market education than DispatchTalon.
- "Field" is crowded in field-service software.

- LIFTIQ / LiftIQ / Lift IQ / LiftOps / LiftReady / LiftGrid: too close to the current risk area and crane/lift descriptors.

- Compliance, Safe, Permit, Legal, Engineer, WHS, RoadLegal, or similar naming: prohibited by product boundary.
